# 🌍 Module 1 — What Is AI, Really?

> **📋 Parent note:** This module needs almost no technology. The goal is intuition, not jargon. The single most important idea your child should walk away with: *traditional computer programs follow rules a human wrote; AI learns patterns from examples.* Everything else builds on that.

## Lesson 1.1 — Intelligence, and Can a Machine Have It?

### 🧠 The Big Idea
"Artificial Intelligence" means **machines doing things that normally need human-like thinking** — recognizing a face, understanding a sentence, winning a game, suggesting a song.

But here's a secret: AI isn't one thing, and it isn't magic. A calculator is incredibly fast at math, but we don't call it "intelligent" because it just follows fixed rules. What makes modern AI special is that it can **learn from experience** instead of being told every single rule.

Think about how *you* learned to tell a cat from a dog. Nobody gave you a rulebook ("if whiskers + pointy ears + says meow = cat"). You just saw lots of cats and lots of dogs, and your brain figured out the pattern. **That's the heart of modern AI.**

### 🎮 Do It: The "Robot Rules" Challenge (no computer needed)
1. Write down step-by-step instructions for a robot to make a peanut butter sandwich. Be *extremely* literal.
2. Have a parent act as the robot and follow your instructions **exactly** — no using common sense. (Did the robot try to spread peanut butter with no knife? Put the jar on the bread unopened?)
3. Lesson learned: writing every rule by hand is *really hard*. Now imagine writing rules for "recognize any cat in any photo." Impossible! That's why we need AI that learns.

### 💬 Think About It
- Is a thermostat "intelligent"? A self-driving car? Where's the line?
- Name three things humans do easily that would be almost impossible to write as rules.

## Lesson 1.2 — AI Is Already All Around You

### 🧠 The Big Idea
You use AI dozens of times a day without noticing. AI is the:
- **Recommendation engine** that picks your next YouTube video or Spotify song
- **Face unlock** on a phone
- **Voice assistant** (Siri, Alexa, Google)
- **Auto-correct** and predictive text on your keyboard
- **Photo app** that groups pictures of the same person
- **Game opponents** that adapt to how you play
- **Spam filter** in email
- **Maps app** predicting traffic and the fastest route

### 🎮 Do It: AI Scavenger Hunt
Over one day, keep a list (paper or notes app) of every time you think AI helped you. Aim for 10. Next to each, write one guess about *what pattern it learned* (e.g., "YouTube learned I like science videos because I watch them to the end").

### 💬 Think About It
- Which of these AIs do you trust the most? The least? Why?
- A recommendation feed shows you more of what you already like. Could that ever be a problem?

## Lesson 1.3 — The Two Kinds of "Smart" Programs

### 🧠 The Big Idea
There are two big ways to make a computer do something clever:

1. **Rule-based ("good old-fashioned") programs** — a human writes out every rule. Great for things with clear rules (a calculator, a thermostat, the logic in a board game).
2. **Machine Learning (ML)** — instead of writing the rules, you show the computer thousands of examples and it *discovers the rules itself.* Great for messy, real-world things (recognizing speech, photos, handwriting).

Almost everything people call "AI" today is really **Machine Learning**. So that's where we're headed next.

### 🎮 Do It: Sort the Tasks
For each task below, decide: **Rules** or **Learning**?
- Convert dollars to euros
- Recognize your grandma's voice on a phone call
- Decide if an email is spam
- Calculate the area of a rectangle
- Tell whether a photo contains a hotdog 🌭

*(Answers: Rules, Learning, Learning, Rules, Learning — anything fuzzy and example-based leans toward Learning.)*

### 🏆 Module 1 Check
You can now (1) explain AI in one sentence, (2) spot AI in daily life, and (3) tell the difference between rule-based programs and machine learning. **Level 1 complete!**
