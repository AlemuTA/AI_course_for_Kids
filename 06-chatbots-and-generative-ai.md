# 💬 Module 6 — Chatbots & Generative AI

> **📋 Parent note — please read:** Most AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot) officially require users to be **13 or older**, and some require 18 with parental consent. Since your child is 12, do these lessons **together on your account, with you present.** This is also a perfect, natural moment to teach digital judgment: not believing everything an AI says, never sharing personal information, and using AI as a thinking *partner*, not a homework-cheating shortcut.

## Lesson 6.1 — How Does ChatGPT Actually Work?

### 🧠 The Big Idea
Chatbots like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are **Large Language Models (LLMs).** Here's the genuinely surprising truth about how they work:

**They are super-powered autocomplete.** 🤯

That's not a joke. An LLM was trained on a giant chunk of the internet and books, and its one core skill is **predicting the next word** in a sentence, over and over, incredibly well. Ask it a question and it generates a sensible answer one word at a time, each word chosen because it "fits" what came before.

It has read so much that this simple trick produces essays, code, poems, and explanations. But remember: **it's predicting plausible words, not looking up guaranteed facts.** That single idea explains both its magic *and* its mistakes.

### 🎮 Do It: Watch the Autocomplete
Type a phrase like "The capital of France is" into a chatbot and watch it complete it. Then try something it can't know, like "My secret password is" — it'll *make something up*, because it's predicting, not knowing.

### 💬 Think About It
- If an AI predicts words rather than knowing facts, why should you *always double-check* important information it gives you?

## Lesson 6.2 — Hallucinations: When AI Confidently Makes Stuff Up

### 🧠 The Big Idea
Sometimes an AI states something false **with total confidence.** This is called a **hallucination.** It happens because the model is generating plausible-sounding words, and sometimes the most "plausible-sounding" answer just isn't true.

This is the #1 reason to treat AI as a *smart but unreliable assistant* — brilliant for brainstorming, drafting, and explaining, but never the final word on facts. **Always verify with a trusted source.**

### 🎮 Do It: Catch a Hallucination
Ask a chatbot something obscure or made-up, like "Tell me about the 1987 World Championship of Competitive Sandwich Stacking." See if it invents details. Then ask it, "Are you sure that's real?" Notice how it often backtracks. Great lesson in healthy skepticism.

### 💬 Think About It
- Why is it dangerous to copy an AI's answer straight into homework without checking it?

## Lesson 6.3 — Becoming a Prompt Wizard 🪄

### 🧠 The Big Idea
A **prompt** is what you type to an AI. The quality of your prompt hugely changes the quality of the answer. Good prompting is a real, valuable skill. The recipe:

- **Be specific.** "Write a story" → "Write a 200-word funny story about a robot who's afraid of vacuum cleaners, for a 10-year-old."
- **Give a role.** "You are a patient science teacher. Explain gravity to a beginner."
- **Show an example** of what you want.
- **Ask for steps.** "Explain it step by step."
- **Iterate.** If the answer isn't right, tell it what to change. Conversations beat one-shot questions.

### 🎮 Do It: The Prompt Battle
Pick one task (e.g., "explain how rainbows form"). Write a *lazy* prompt and a *great* prompt. Compare the two answers side by side. Then keep improving your great prompt three more times. You'll *feel* the skill develop.

### 💬 Think About It
- Generative AI also makes **images, music, and video** from prompts. If anyone can generate a realistic image of anything... how do we know what's real online? (Hold that thought — it's the heart of Module 7.)

### 🏆 Module 6 Check
You understand what LLMs really are, why they hallucinate, and how to prompt like a pro. You can now use AI *wisely.* 🧙
